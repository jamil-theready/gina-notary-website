## What Does a Loan Signing Agent Do?

A loan signing agent is a notary public who specializes in real estate closings. Their job is straightforward. They meet with borrowers, walk them through the loan document package, witness signatures, and notarize the documents that require it.

This is the final step before your mortgage funds and your transaction closes. The signing agent is the person who puts the paperwork in your hands and makes sure everything is executed correctly.

Not every notary handles loan signings. It takes specific training to manage a stack of 100 to 150 pages of mortgage documents. A general notary might notarize a power of attorney or an affidavit. A [loan signing agent](/notary-services/loan-signing-agent-sacramento/) handles the entire closing package from start to finish.

## How a Loan Signing Agent Differs from a Regular Notary

Every loan signing agent is a notary. Not every notary is a loan signing agent.

A regular notary public verifies identity and witnesses signatures on individual documents. They may handle one or two pages at a time. A loan signing agent works with complete mortgage packages that include dozens of documents requiring precise execution.

The difference comes down to specialization. A signing agent knows what a deed of trust looks like. They can spot a missing initial or an unsigned rider before the package goes back to the lender. That attention to detail prevents costly delays in your closing.

## California Requirements for Loan Signing Agents

California has specific standards for notaries who handle loan signings. Here is what the state requires:

- **Active notary commission** issued by the California Secretary of State
- **Background check** through the Department of Justice and FBI
- **Notary bond** of $15,000 minimum
- **Errors and omissions insurance** (strongly recommended and often required by signing services)
- **Completion of a loan signing agent course** covering mortgage documents and closing procedures

These requirements exist to protect borrowers. When you hire a qualified signing agent, you know they have been vetted and trained to handle your documents properly.

## Common Documents in a Loan Signing Package

During your signing appointment, you will encounter several key documents:

**Deed of Trust** is the document that secures the loan against your property. It names the borrower, the lender, and the trustee. This document gets recorded with the county.

**HUD-1 Settlement Statement** (or Closing Disclosure on newer loans) breaks down every cost in the transaction. It shows your loan amount, interest rate, closing costs, and what you owe at signing.

**Right to Cancel Notice** applies to refinance transactions. Federal law gives you three business days to cancel a refinance after signing. The signing agent makes sure you understand this right and sign the acknowledgment.

**Signature and Name Affidavit** confirms your identity and any variations of your name that may appear in public records.

**Compliance Agreement** states that you will cooperate if any documents need to be re-signed due to errors.

These are just the highlights. A typical package contains many more forms, riders, and disclosures.

## What to Bring to Your Loan Signing Appointment

Preparation makes the appointment go smoothly. Here is what you need:

- **Valid government issued photo ID** such as a California driver's license, passport, or state ID card. The name on your ID must match the name on the loan documents.
- **A second form of ID** if requested by the title company.
- **Any cashier's checks** required for closing costs (your escrow officer will tell you the exact amount in advance).
- **Reading glasses** if you need them. You will be reading and signing a lot of pages.

Do not sign anything before the appointment. Every signature must be witnessed by the notary.
