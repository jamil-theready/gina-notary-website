## What Does a Real Estate Closing Notary Do?

A real estate closing notary serves as a crucial, impartial witness for your property transactions. My main responsibility is to verify the identity of each signer. I ensure they are signing documents willingly and are aware of what they sign. This process protects all parties involved in a real estate deal.

My role is not about providing legal advice. My job is to prevent fraud. I confirm that the individuals signing a deed or loan agreement are indeed who they claim to be. This builds trust and security into the entire closing process.

## Why is a Notary Essential for California Real Estate Transactions?

California law mandates notarization for many real estate documents. This legal requirement protects property owners and buyers alike. For instance, deeds transferring property title must be notarized. This applies to Grant Deeds, Quitclaim Deeds, and Deeds of Trust. Without proper notarization, these documents may not be recorded. They could also face legal challenges down the road.

California Government Code Section 8202 specifically outlines a notary public's duties. It emphasizes the need to verify the identity of the signer. I must obtain satisfactory evidence of identity from each person signing. This involves examining valid, government issued identification. This strict adherence to state law safeguards your property rights. ## What Documents Require Notarization During a Property Closing?

Many documents during a real estate closing need notarization. These are the instruments that formally transfer ownership or secure financial obligations.

Key documents I frequently notarize include:

*   **Grant Deeds:** These transfer property ownership from seller to buyer.
*   **Quitclaim Deeds:** These transfer interest in a property without warranty of title. You can read more about the differences in my post, [California Grant Deed vs. Quitclaim Deed Explained](/blog/california-grant-deed-vs-quitclaim-deed-explained/).
*   **Deeds of Trust or Mortgages:** These documents secure a loan against the property.
*   **Promissory Notes:** These are promises to repay a loan.
*   **Loan Agreements:** Detailed contracts outlining loan terms.
*   **Affidavits:** Sworn statements of fact.
*   **Escrow Instructions:** Directives to the escrow holder.
*   **Power of Attorney:** If someone signs on behalf of another. Learn more about this service on my [Power of Attorney Notary Guide](/blog/power-of-attorney-notary-sacramento-guide/).

Each signature on these documents confirms the signer's agreement to the terms. My notarization provides legal authentication.
