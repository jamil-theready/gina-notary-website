## The Short Answer

A demand letter is a formal written request asking someone to pay a debt, return property, fix a problem, or fulfill a legal obligation. You send it before filing a lawsuit. It puts the other party on notice that you are serious and gives them a chance to resolve the issue without court involvement.

While notarization is not required by California law, having your demand letter notarized creates a verified record. Courts look favorably on documentation that shows you took the dispute seriously and followed proper procedures.

## When You Need a Demand Letter

Demand letters are used across a wide range of disputes in Sacramento. The common thread is that someone owes you something and is not delivering.

### Unpaid Debts

Someone owes you money and has stopped responding. This could be a personal loan, an unpaid invoice for services, or money owed from a verbal agreement. A demand letter formalizes the request and starts a paper trail.

### Security Deposit Disputes

California law requires landlords to return security deposits within 21 days of a tenant moving out. If your landlord has not returned your deposit or has made improper deductions, a demand letter is the standard first step before filing in small claims court.

### Property Damage

A neighbor's tree fell on your fence. A contractor damaged your property during a job. Someone hit your parked car and is not paying for repairs. A demand letter documents the damage and requests compensation.

### Breach of Contract

A contractor did not finish the work. A business did not deliver what was promised. A demand letter references the contract terms, identifies the breach, and states what you expect.

### Returned Checks and Failed Payments

California Civil Code Section 1719 allows you to recover up to three times the amount of a bounced check. A demand letter is required before you can collect treble damages.

## Why Notarization Matters

A demand letter works without notarization. But notarization makes it stronger in three ways.

**Verified identity.** The notary confirms that you, the sender, are who you say you are. This eliminates any argument that someone else wrote or signed the letter.

**Official record.** The notary's seal and journal entry create an independent record of the signing. If the other party claims they never received a legitimate demand letter, you have proof.

**Court credibility.** Judges in Sacramento County small claims court see thousands of cases. A notarized demand letter signals that you documented the dispute properly. It shows preparation, and prepared parties tend to be taken more seriously.

## How to Write an Effective Demand Letter

Keep it professional, factual, and specific. Courts do not respond well to emotional language or threats.

### Start With the Facts

State who you are, who the letter is addressed to, and what happened. Include dates, locations, and any relevant reference numbers such as invoice numbers or contract dates.

### State What You Want

Be specific about the resolution you are requesting. If the other party owes you $3,200 for unpaid work, say so. If you want your security deposit returned, state the exact amount. Vague demands weaken your position.

### Set a Deadline

Give the other party a reasonable timeframe to respond. Ten to fourteen days is standard for straightforward money disputes. Thirty days is appropriate for complex issues.

### Reference the Legal Consequences

State that you will pursue legal remedies if the matter is not resolved by the deadline. You can mention small claims court or civil court, but avoid language that sounds like a threat. A simple statement such as "I will pursue all available legal remedies" is sufficient.

### Sign and Notarize

Sign the letter in front of a notary public. The notary verifies your identity, witnesses your signature, and applies their official seal.

## Sending the Demand Letter

How you send the letter matters as much as what it says.

**Certified mail with return receipt.** USPS Certified Mail creates a tracking record. The return receipt (green card) proves that someone at the address received the letter. This is the standard method for demand letters in California.

**Keep the originals.** Store the notarized original, a copy of the letter, the certified mail receipt, and the return receipt together. If you file a lawsuit, these documents go with you to court.

## Sacramento Small Claims Court

If the demand letter does not resolve the dispute, the next step is Sacramento County Small Claims Court.

**Location:** Sacramento County Superior Court, 720 9th Street, Sacramento, CA 95814

**Claim limits:** Up to $12,500 for individuals. Up to $6,250 for businesses, government entities, and other organizations.

**Filing fee:** $30 to $75 depending on the claim amount.

**What to bring:** Your notarized demand letter, proof of certified mail delivery, the return receipt, any contracts or invoices, and photos or other evidence supporting your claim.

Sacramento small claims judges often ask whether you attempted to resolve the dispute before filing. Your notarized demand letter and certified mail receipt answer that question clearly.
